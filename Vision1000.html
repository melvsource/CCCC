Executive Summary

The Vision in Full

Vision 1,000 is the pursuit of a Christ-centered community of one thousand visible and available disciples whose lives are being transformed by the gospel of Jesus Christ, faithfully cultivating and guarding all that God has entrusted to them, so that Christ may be glorified in them, through them, and beyond them.

THE VISION STATEMENT
The Vision
Christ — and His glory revealed through transformed lives
The Mission
Disciple-making through the Great Commission, compelled by the love of Christ received
The Framework
Abad & Shamar — faithfully cultivating and guarding all God entrusts, across every domain of life
The Number
A visible measure and practical boundary — not the goal itself. The goal is Christ.
The Outcome
A flourishing community bearing witness to God's glory — testimony, not achievement
The Movement
Leaders raised, disciples sent, communities planted — success measured by faithfulness, not retention
01 · Biblical Foundations

The Scriptural Architecture

Three pillars hold everything. Remove any one and the structure collapses. Together they define what CCCC is, how it operates, and whose it is.

Community Photo
Scripture Study
01

Creation order: Abad & Shamar — fulfilled in Christ

Gen. 2:15 · 2 Cor. 3:18 · Col. 1:10

God entrusted humanity with the vocation of cultivating and guarding all that He had given, and humanity abandoned it. What Adam failed to keep, Christ came to fulfill — the true and faithful image-bearer who cultivated perfectly and guarded completely, all the way to the cross and through it. Now, by union with Him, those who belong to Christ are being continuously transformed into that same image, from one degree of glory to another. Abad and Shamar is therefore not a command reimposed on disciples from the outside. It is the shape of the life Christ has freed us into.

02

Redemption as re-ordering — the Psalm 107 pattern

Ps. 107:35–38

Psalm 107 does not open with a program. It opens with a cry — and then with God answering it. The redeemed are gathered from every direction: from hunger and thirst in the desert, from darkness and chains, from affliction and near-death, from the crashing sea. What follows is never self-improvement — it is transformation by mercy. And then in verses 35–38: "He turned the desert into pools of water and the parched ground into flowing springs; there he brought the hungry to live, and they founded a city where they could settle. They sowed fields and planted vineyards that yielded a fruitful harvest; he blessed them, and their numbers greatly increased." This is the vision image for CCCC. A wilderness made fruitful. Hungry people given a city. Fields planted — which means there is work to be done; the redeemed do not sit idle. Harvests yielded — which means God is the one blessing the work for His purposes, and we are not simply thriving on our own terms. Numbers multiplied — not through strategy, but through the overflow of divine mercy. What emerges — order, fruitfulness, a city founded — is not community development. It is doxology: the visible overflow of what God has done in those He has redeemed.

"He turned the desert into pools of water and the parched ground into flowing springs; there he brought the hungry to live, and they founded a city where they could settle."

PSALM 107:35–36
03

The church's constitution and commission

Matt. 16:18 · Matt. 28:18–20 · Acts 1:8

Matthew 16:18 establishes ownership: the church belongs to Christ, and He is its builder. Matthew 28:18–20 establishes operation: because all authority belongs to Him, the community He builds is sent — to go, to baptize, to teach obedience to everything He commanded. Acts 1:8 names the only sufficient power: not organizational capacity or vision clarity, but the Holy Spirit. All framework categories are servants of this commission, never its substitutes.

02 · Theological Vision

What CCCC Believes It Is Doing

Before any program, any structure, any number — CCCC operates from a set of theological convictions. These are not aspirations. They are the load-bearing walls.

01

A doxological event, not an organizational achievement

Matt. 16:18 · Rev. 7:9–10 · Col. 1:10 · Heb. 12:2

When Vision 1,000 is realized, it will only be a testimony and a witness of God's glory. The 1,000 is not a trophy — it is a community of a thousand transformed lives. Not an organizational achievement, but a doxological event: a glimpse, here and now, of the great multitude from every nation that will one day worship together before the throne of God (Rev. 7:9–10). A living preview of the eschatological reality that Christ is building toward — the gates of Hades cannot prevail against it (Matt. 16:18).

The 1,000 is a number manifesting the power of the gospel as unstoppable. It places the church firmly on the offensive — not gathered into a defended fortress, but advancing. Every transformed life is a data point in God's case against despair and unbelief. Together, a thousand such lives become an argument impossible to ignore.

The goal was never institutional success. The goal was always the glory of God made visible through changed lives — disciples loving one another (John 13:35), abiding in Christ (John 15:4–5), living worthy of the Lord in every good work (Col. 1:10), looking to Jesus the Author and Perfecter of faith (Heb. 12:2). CCCC, if it succeeds, will have nothing to show for itself that is its own. Everything will belong to the one who built it.

"The least of you will become a thousand, the smallest a mighty nation. I am the Lord; in its time I will do this swiftly."

ISAIAH 60:22 · The vision's animating promise
02

The conviction behind CCCC's love for one another

John 13:35 · John 15:4–5

The love that CCCC is called to embody is not a program or a culture initiative — it is the direct fruit of abiding in Christ. Jesus declared in John 15:4–5: "Remain in me, as I also remain in you. No branch can bear fruit by itself; it must remain in the vine. Neither can you bear fruit unless you remain in me… apart from me you can do nothing." This abiding is the root. The love visible to the world is the fruit. And in John 13:35: "By this everyone will know that you are my disciples, if you love one another." CCCC's community life is the natural overflow evidence that its people are truly abiding in Christ. The only sustainable love is abiding love.

"By this everyone will know that you are my disciples, if you love one another."

JOHN 13:35

04

No named founder: a structural theological conviction

1 Cor. 3:5–7 · Heb. 10:24–25

The anonymity of founding leaders is not humility performed — it is a structural theological stance against personality-driven church culture. CCCC's founders are identified not by name but by disposition: those who loved the Lord, kept meeting together, and agreed in humility that this vision was planted by God — not conceived by any one of them.

03 · Discipleship Philosophy

How CCCC Forms People

Formation is the irreducible work. Everything else — programs, numbers, structures — is scaffolding. The building is people becoming like Christ.

01

Visible and available disciples

Matt. 28:19–20 · Heb. 10:24–25

Vision 1,000 does not count attendees or spectators. It seeks visible and available disciples — those who deliberately place themselves within the ordinary means through which God works: His Word, His people, prayer, fellowship, teaching, correction, encouragement, and service. The church's responsibility is to faithfully create the conditions where people may continually encounter Christ.

02

The overflow model

Col. 3:16 · John 21:15–17 · John 13:35

Discipleship flows from the inside out: personal devotion to Jesus overflows into community life, which overflows into the world. The love that marks CCCC is not manufactured warmth — it is the overflow of abiding in Christ (John 15:4–5), made visible in the way members genuinely care for one another, and ultimately unmistakable to those outside (John 13:35).

03

Two tracks: spiritual and practical

Eph. 4:28 · Prov. 3:5–6 · 1 Tim. 4:7

Elders oversee spiritual formation. Deacons oversee practical formation — answering the "how" of Ephesians 4:28, pushing the wisdom of Proverbs 22:29 and 14:23 (skillful, profitable work that stands before those in need), with the conviction of Ecclesiastes 9:10 (whatever your hand finds to do, do it with all your might). The two tracks are not parallel programs but a single integrated life: practical wisdom is never severed from its theological root, and theological conviction is always tested in its practical expression.

04

The 1,000 as discipleship ecology — and doxological boundary

Ex. 18:21–22 · Prov. 27:17 · Rev. 7:9–10

The limit of 1,000 creates manageable shepherding — every member known, every soul accountable, every gift locatable. The 1,000 is not a membership ceiling; it is a discipleship boundary — a safeguard against ungovernable scale where genuine formation becomes impossible. But the boundary is also theological: large enough to be a visible, compelling witness and small enough to remain a real community where names are known. At the threshold, the vision does not end. It multiplies. The ceiling becomes a womb.

04 · Sustainable Livelihood Programs

Answering the How of Ephesians 4:28

Ephesians 4:28 · Proverbs 22:29 · Proverbs 14:23 · Ecclesiastes 9:10 — The Organizing Principle

"Anyone who has been stealing must steal no more, but must work, doing something useful with their own hands, that they may have something to share with those in need."

A complete theology of economic transformation in three movements: stop (repentance made possible only by the finished work of Christ), work (diligent, skillful labor as worship — "Do you see someone skilled in their work? They will serve before kings," Prov. 22:29), and share (giving out of the overflow of Christ's love, not moral duty alone). Two further anchors shape the posture: "All hard work brings a profit, but mere talk leads only to poverty" (Prov. 14:23), and "Whatever your hand finds to do, do it with all your might" (Ecc. 9:10). These three movements are not a morality program. Without the finished work of Christ, Stop produces only guilt, Work produces only pride, and Share produces only performance. It is Christ's atoning work that enables true repentance, His resurrection that empowers reverent labor, and the overflow of His love — not obligation — that makes genuine sharing possible.

Stop

True repentance — not behavior management, but the response of a heart encountered by Christ. Made possible only by His finished work on the cross. Biblical accountability through discipleship groups and elder counsel. 2 Cor. 5:17 · Rom. 6:1–4

Work

Dignified, skillful labor as worship — "skilled in their work… will serve before kings" (Prov. 22:29). Not hustle for survival but stewardship before God. Every program develops capacity. Abad in action. Prov. 22:29 · Ecc. 9:10 · Col. 3:23

Share

Giving not from surplus alone but from the overflow of Christ's love received — "we love because He first loved us" (1 John 4:19). Those equipped become equippers. Those employed become employers. Shamar as doxology, not duty. 1 John 4:19 · 2 Cor. 9:7

A formation crucible for Elders and emerging leaders. Character precedes role. Those who complete this track are not graduates — they are men and women proven by time, tested by trial, trusted by the community.
  • 1 Timothy 3 and Titus 1 character pathway

Track D
Sending School — Pre-Deployment Formation
Activates as the community approaches its multiplication threshold. Prepares leaders and families for planting a new community — missiology, pioneer hardship, and the theology of sending.
  • Theology of sending: biblical missiology (Acts 13)
  • Pioneer community formation principles

  • Spiritual warfare and resilience in isolation

Acts 13:1–3 · Matt. 28:19 · 2 Tim. 2:2 · Isa. 60:22

The integration principle: one school, four gates

Eph. 4:11–13 · 2 Tim. 3:16–17

A child formed in Track A enters Track B as a young adult. A theologically formed member is identified for Track C. A proven elder enters Track D when the sending season arrives. The school is not a department of the church — it is the church's generation-spanning work of equipping every member, until the whole body reaches "the whole measure of the fullness of Christ" (Eph. 4:13).

Track B begins first. Tracks A, C, and D follow in sequence as the community reaches capacity and spiritual maturity to sustain them.

06 · Organizational Structure

The Architecture of the Vision

Every structural choice CCCC makes encodes a theological conviction — about power, about people, about who the church ultimately belongs to.

Facilitating Pastor
Unifies Elders & Deacons. Fulltime. Cycles to Elder/Deacon role after set years. Sabbatical provision allowed.
12 Elders
Ministers of the Word. Prayer, teaching, shepherding, counsel. Ideally fulltime. Spiritual oversight.
12 Deacons
Servants of the people. Max 4 fulltime. Practical needs, livelihood programs, community care.
Members — Vision 1,000
Ages 21–60 counted. Those 65+ are honored beneficiaries and active teachers of the next generation (Titus 2:2–5).

Anti-institutionalization mechanisms

Mark 10:42–45 · 1 Pet. 5:3

The Pastor's rotation prevents permanent top authority. Anonymous founding prevents personality cults. The 1,000 ceiling prevents ungovernable scale. Each is a structural expression of the conviction that no leader lords it over the flock — built in, not merely aspirational.

Divided responsibilities

1 Cor. 3:6–7

The church remains responsible for faithfulness. God remains responsible for transformation. This theological division of labor prevents both passivity and pride. CCCC plants and waters. The increase belongs to God alone.

08 · Multiplication Strategy

Sending as Love, Not Loss

The 1,000 is not a wall. It is a womb. When the community approaches its limit, it does not contract — it reproduces.

Sending Moment / Community Photo
01

The logic of the limit

Acts 13:1–3 · Isa. 60:22

Leaders formed within the Vision 1,000 ecology are identified, affirmed, and commissioned — exactly as the church at Antioch sent Paul and Barnabas. "The least of you will become a thousand, the smallest a mighty nation." Isaiah 60:22 is not merely a cover verse — it is the eschatological logic beneath the multiplication mandate.

02

Full independence with open hands

Gal. 2:9–10 · Phil. 1:3–5 · Rom. 14:5

Sent-out communities are fully independent — no structural authority retained by CCCC over them. They may follow the Vision 1,000 pattern or discern their own form under the Spirit's leading; both are held loosely. This is not indifference — it is theological conviction. The Spirit works variously (1 Cor. 12:4–6), and a tested pattern is a gift offered, never a law enforced.

Preserved Tension: Working Structure vs. Spirit-Led Freedom

Scripture does not prescribe a single ecclesial form. CCCC holds its own pattern as a gift it received — not a law it imposes on those it sends. Sent communities may adapt or depart from the Vision 1,000 model. This tension between a tested working structure and the Spirit's freedom operating differently in different people and places is left deliberately unresolved. It is not a weakness in the framework — it is faithfulness to what Scripture itself does not settle. What must never be compromised is the foundation: Christ and Him crucified. What must never be corrupted is the motive: love for Christ and for the people He purchased with His blood. Rom. 14:5 · 1 Cor. 12:4–6 · Gal. 5:1

Support that demands compliance is not support. Structure that cannot release is not sending — it is franchising. And a church that sends only to extend its own influence has confused the kingdom of God with its own kingdom.

03

What carries forward — and what it is built on

2 Tim. 2:2 · Col. 2:6–7 · 1 Cor. 13:1–3

What CCCC exports is not a model but people — men and women rooted in Christ, trained in Abad and Shamar, marked by the Great Commission. But the relationship that continues between a sending community and those it has sent must be examined carefully as to what it is built on.

It is not built on mutual benefit — as though the connection persists only while it is useful to both parties. It is not built on civil peace — the desire to avoid friction or maintain comfortable association. It is not built on shared comfort — the warmth of familiar faces and shared history. All of these things may be present, and none of them are wrong. But none of them are the foundation.

The only foundation that outlasts every organizational structure, every geographical distance, every difference in form and method, is the love of Christ — received, not generated. "We love because He first loved us" (1 John 4:19). What was built together was built by His grace. What continues is sustained by His love. What is sent is sent in His name. If that foundation is removed, the relationship becomes a network. And a network, however useful, is not the Body of Christ.

"And over all these virtues put on love, which binds them all together in perfect unity."

COLOSSIANS 3:14 · The only bond that holds what is sent together with what sent it
